If you've used Teal (or Teal HQ) for more than a few weeks, you probably have the same relationship with it that most job seekers eventually do. It's a clean, well-built tracker. The Chrome extension is genuinely useful. Saving jobs from LinkedIn takes one click. Your applications are finally in one place instead of scattered across a dozen tabs and a half-finished spreadsheet.

And then, after about a month, you start to notice something. Teal tells you *what* you've applied to. It doesn't tell you whether you *should* have applied in the first place.

That gap is why people start searching for a **teal alternative**. This guide walks through what Teal does well, where it falls short in 2026, and why a fit-first tool like WorthApply is a better fit for people who are tired of applying into the void.

## What Teal Does and Who It's For

Teal's core product is a job application tracker with a few bolt-on features. You install the Chrome extension, and whenever you see a role on LinkedIn, Indeed, or a company careers page, you can save it to your Teal dashboard with one click.

From there, Teal gives you:

- A pipeline view of every role you've saved, with status stages (Bookmarked, Applied, Interviewing, Offer, Rejected)
- A resume builder that lets you create multiple resume versions
- A "Match Score" that compares your resume to a specific job description
- Contact management for people you're networking with
- A cover letter generator

The audience is clear: active job seekers who apply to a lot of roles and need help staying organized. If you're submitting fifteen to thirty applications a week and losing track of which company emailed you back, Teal is a serious upgrade over a Notion page or a spreadsheet.

That's the best version of Teal. It's a good application tracker.

## Teal's Limitations: Tracking-First, Not Fit-First

The problem isn't that Teal does tracking poorly. It's that tracking is the *last* step of the job search, not the first. And Teal's product design assumes you already know which jobs to apply to.

Here's what that looks like in practice.

You're scrolling LinkedIn. You see a Senior Product Manager role at a mid-stage fintech. It looks interesting. You click the Teal extension and save it. Teal now tracks that role. But Teal doesn't tell you:

- Whether your actual experience matches what the role requires
- What specific gaps are likely to get your resume filtered out
- Whether the role's seniority level is realistic for your background
- Whether the skills you have transfer, or whether the job description is coded for a different kind of candidate
- Whether this is a role you should invest the hour it takes to tailor a resume for

Teal's Match Score tries to fill some of that gap, but it's essentially a keyword-overlap count. It compares words on your resume to words in the JD. If the JD says "Python" and your resume says "Python," your score goes up. If the JD says "product-led growth" and your resume doesn't, your score goes down — even if you led PLG initiatives and just used different wording.

Keyword matching is a useful *proxy* for fit, but it's not the same thing. Two resumes can have identical Match Scores and completely different chances of getting an interview, because keyword overlap doesn't capture seniority calibration, scope mismatch, or whether your experience actually solves the problem the employer is hiring for.

The second limitation is philosophical. Teal encourages volume. The pipeline view is satisfying to fill. Every saved role feels like progress. But a dashboard of 200 roles you're "tracking" isn't the same as 10 roles you're seriously pursuing — and the 200-role version leads to burnout, fatigue, and the classic symptom of ghosting despite constant effort.

## When Teal Is Still the Right Choice

To be fair: there are cases where Teal is the better tool.

If you're a high-volume applicant by design — recruiting-adjacent roles, sales roles where volume correlates with results, or contract work where you genuinely are willing to take most things — then Teal's tracking-first model matches your actual workflow. Fit analysis would slow you down because you've already decided you're going wide.

If you're deep into a search and your main problem is organizational chaos, not application quality, Teal solves the organizational problem well.

If you specifically want a tool that feels like Notion but for job hunting, Teal's UI is excellent and the contact management is a real differentiator.

Teal is a good tool. It's just answering a different question than the one most job seekers need answered.

## Other Alternatives Worth Considering

Teal and WorthApply aren't the only options. A few others are worth knowing.

- **Huntr** — Closest direct competitor to Teal on tracking. Similar Chrome extension flow, similar pipeline UI. Weaker on AI features. Good if you want Teal but prefer a different interface.
- **Jobscan** — Pure ATS keyword optimization. No tracking, no fit analysis. Useful if your *only* problem is passing the ATS filter on roles you've already decided to apply to.
- **Rezi** — AI resume builder. Strong at generating polished bullets, weaker on fit evaluation. Higher fabrication risk because it generates rather than edits.
- **Notion or a Google Sheet** — The DIY option. Free, infinitely customizable, zero intelligence. Works well for disciplined people who don't need software nudges.

Most of these compete with Teal, not WorthApply, because they're all tracking-or-optimization tools. The fit-first category is genuinely small right now.

## FAQ

### Is Teal still worth using in 2026?

Yes, if you're using it for its core strength: application tracking. It remains one of the better trackers available and the free tier covers the essentials. The Match Score and AI features are less differentiated than they were in 2023 — if those are the features you care about, other tools do them better.

### What's the main difference between Teal and WorthApply?

Teal tracks applications and optimizes keywords. WorthApply evaluates whether you should apply at all, then helps you tailor your materials if the answer is yes. Teal's starting question is "did I apply?" WorthApply's is "should I apply?"

### Can I use Teal and WorthApply together?

Yes, and some people do. Run the fit analysis in WorthApply to decide which roles are worth pursuing, then log them in Teal to track your pipeline. The combined cost is higher than picking one, but the workflows are complementary rather than redundant.

### Does WorthApply have a Chrome extension like Teal's?

WorthApply's extension is more narrowly scoped. It pulls job descriptions for analysis rather than saving them to a pipeline. If one-click job saving is the feature you most care about, Teal's extension is more mature.

### Is the WorthApply Match Score the same as Teal's?

No. Teal's Match Score is a keyword-overlap calculation. WorthApply's fit score weights multiple dimensions — skills, experience calibration, seniority fit, and scope — rather than just counting shared words. Two resumes can have identical keyword overlap and very different fit scores.
