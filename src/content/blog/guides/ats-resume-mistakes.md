You tailored your resume, rewrote your bullet points, and submitted the application. Three weeks pass. Nothing. You check the portal — still "under review." The quiet truth is that a human might never have opened your file. An Applicant Tracking System looked at it first, flagged something it couldn't parse or didn't match, and moved on.

ATS systems are not the villain job-search content makes them out to be. They're not rejecting you on purpose. They're parsing tools trying to extract structured data from an unstructured document, and small formatting or content choices can make that parsing fail. When it fails, your resume often never gets surfaced to a recruiter — not because you weren't qualified, but because the system couldn't read you clearly.

This guide walks through the seven mistakes we see most often, in order of how badly they sabotage applications. At the end, we'll cover the mistake that matters more than all of them combined: applying to jobs where you're not a real fit in the first place.

## What ATS Systems Actually Do (and Don't Do)

Before the mistakes, a quick reality check. An ATS is not a gatekeeper with a secret scoring formula. It's a database that recruiters use to organize incoming applications. When you submit a resume, the ATS:

- Parses the file into structured fields (name, email, work history, skills, education)
- Indexes the text so recruiters can search it by keyword
- Ranks or filters candidates based on criteria the recruiter sets

It does NOT, in most systems, automatically reject your resume because you missed a keyword. What actually happens is more mundane and more damaging: the recruiter runs a search like "Python AND Kubernetes AND 5 years experience," and if the parser misread your file, you don't appear in their results. You weren't rejected. You were invisible.

That distinction matters for everything that follows.

## Mistake 1: Using Headers and Footers for Key Info

Many ATS parsers skip content inside Word or PDF headers and footers. That's a problem because a common resume template puts your name, phone number, and email in the header.

When the parser strips the header, you end up as a nameless, contactless record in the recruiter's database. Even if they find your resume in a search, they can't reach you.

Fix: Move your name and contact information into the body of the document, at the top of the first page. Treat the header and footer as optional decoration, not as places to store anything important.

## Mistake 2: Tables and Columns That Break Parsing

Two-column resume layouts look clean in a PDF viewer. They are also one of the top causes of garbled ATS output. When a parser reads a two-column document, it often reads left column all the way down, then right column all the way down, mixing your job titles with dates from unrelated roles.

The same problem applies to tables used for skills or experience grids. Some modern parsers handle them better than they used to, but you have no way of knowing which system the employer uses.

Fix: Use a single-column layout. Put your skills in a simple list, your experience in chronological blocks, and your education in its own section. Save visual design for the portfolio or LinkedIn profile you link to.

## Mistake 3: Missing Keywords Vs the Job Description

ATS software doesn't read for meaning. It reads for exact matches. If the job description says "Salesforce administration" and your resume says "CRM administration" without ever naming Salesforce, you may not surface in a keyword search.

This is the mistake most guides focus on, and they usually push it too far. Keyword matching matters, but keyword stuffing — repeating phrases unnaturally or listing tools you barely used — backfires the moment a human reads your resume or asks you about it in an interview.

The right approach is to mirror the exact terminology of the job description in places where it genuinely applies to your experience. If you really did use Salesforce, write "Salesforce" instead of "CRM." If you really led cross-functional teams, use that phrase if the job description does.

If you find that very few of the job's keywords apply to your actual experience, that's not a tailoring problem. That's a fit problem, and no amount of keyword engineering will fix it.

## Mistake 4: Wrong File Format

Most ATS systems handle `.docx` and modern text-based `.pdf` files well. Problems start with:

- Image-based PDFs (scanned or exported from a design tool as an image)
- Old `.doc` files from legacy Word versions
- Pages files, Google Docs exports with unusual formatting, or `.rtf`

If your PDF was created by exporting a Canva or Figma design, there's a decent chance the text is rendered as shapes, not as selectable text. A parser can't read it. A human can, which is why the resume looks fine to you when you open it.

Fix: Export to `.docx` when the job posting allows it. If you must submit a PDF, make sure the text is selectable — open the file, try to copy a bullet point, and paste it into a text editor. If you get your words back, the parser will too. If you get nothing or random symbols, export again from a text-based tool like Word or Google Docs.

## Mistake 5: Vague Bullet Points With No Measurable Impact

This one doesn't break the ATS, but it breaks everything that happens after. When a recruiter searches and your resume does appear, they spend maybe six seconds scanning it. Bullet points like "Responsible for managing team projects" tell them nothing.

Strong bullets follow a simple structure: action verb, specific thing you did, measurable outcome. Compare:

- Before: Managed marketing campaigns for a growing SaaS company
- After: Launched 12 multi-channel campaigns that drove a 34% increase in qualified leads over two quarters

The second version is easier for the human to evaluate and also happens to include more keywords naturally. You don't need every bullet to have a number, but at least half should give the reader something concrete to latch onto.

## Mistake 6: Using an Objective Statement Instead of a Summary

An "Objective" section from 15 years ago said things like "Seeking a challenging role where I can grow my skills." That format is dead. Recruiters skim past it. ATS systems index it but don't weight it heavily.

A professional summary does the opposite job. In three to four lines at the top of your resume, it tells the recruiter who you are, what you do, and what you're best at — using the same language the job description uses.

Example for a backend engineer applying to a fintech role:

"Backend engineer with 6 years building payment infrastructure at Stripe and a Series B fintech. Specialize in high-throughput Go services, PostgreSQL optimization, and PCI-compliant system design. Shipped the settlement engine handling $400M in annual transaction volume."

That summary does more work than an objective statement ever did.

## Mistake 7: Applying Without Checking Fit First

This is the mistake that dwarfs the other six combined. You can fix every formatting issue, hit every keyword, and write the cleanest bullets in the world — and still get rejected if the job requires skills you don't have and a seniority level that doesn't match your experience.

Most job seekers find this out the hard way after sending 60 applications into silence. The ATS isn't the problem. The fit isn't there.

Before you spend 45 minutes tailoring a resume for a specific role, spend five minutes asking a harder question: should you be applying to this role at all? That means honestly comparing your years of experience, your core skills, and your domain exposure to what the job description actually requires. If three of the top five requirements are gaps, this is not your job. Save the effort for roles where you have a real shot.

## How to Check Your Resume Against ATS Before Applying

A few things you can do in the next 30 minutes:

1. Copy your entire resume and paste it into a plain text editor. What you see is roughly what the ATS sees. If sections are out of order or contact info is missing, fix the document.
2. Run a word-count on the terms the job description emphasizes. If the job mentions "Python" six times and your resume mentions it twice, and you really do use Python daily, that's an easy tightening.
3. Read your resume out loud. If a bullet point doesn't sound like something a real person would say about their work, rewrite it.
4. Most importantly, before you hit submit, check that you actually match the core requirements of the role. Fit first, format second.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does every company use an ATS?

Most mid-sized and large companies do. Small businesses and startups sometimes review applications manually, but even many 20-person startups now use Greenhouse, Lever, or Ashby. Assume an ATS is in the loop unless you have inside information otherwise.

### Should I use a Word doc or a PDF?

Either works for modern ATS systems, with one caveat: PDFs must be text-based, not image-based. If a job posting specifies a format, use what they ask for. If you're unsure, `.docx` has the widest compatibility.

### Do ATS systems actually reject resumes automatically?

Rarely. Most of them don't auto-reject at all. They rank or filter based on recruiter-defined criteria, and your resume becomes harder to surface if it's poorly parsed or mismatched to the search terms.

### How many keywords should I include from the job description?

Include keywords that genuinely match your experience. There's no magic number. If the job mentions a skill you have, name it exactly the way the posting does. If it mentions a skill you don't have, don't add it — you'll just fail the interview.

### Is a fancy resume template hurting my chances?

Often, yes. Graphic-heavy templates with columns, icons, and sidebars look impressive to you and illegible to a parser. A clean, single-column, text-based layout is the safer default for every application.
